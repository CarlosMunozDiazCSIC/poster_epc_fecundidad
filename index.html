The relation between the parental Mean Absolute Age Difference (MAAD) and the fertility outcomes in Spain

J. Sebastian Ruiz-Santacruz, Cristina Suero, Francisco Viciana, Stanislao Mazzoni and Diego Ramiro Fariñas

May a lower age gap between parents influence fertility outcomes?

Background

Most of the research has been done on the patterns and the relationship between age at first marriage (Esteve, Cortina and Cabré, 2009; Kolk, 2015) and the possible outcomes in fertility (Eurostat, 2018; Rotering and Bras, 2019), little is known about the age differences of parents at the time of their children’s birth (Dudel, Cheng, and Klüsener ,2020) as a possible factor of the low and the lowest low fertility.

Hypothesis

The increment in the MAAD can influence the fertility outcomes in Spain and should be revised to consider it as a factor of the lowest low fertility pattern the country is performing. This will give a disadvantage in the number of children that women can have if they have children with more age difference between their partners (Bongaarts & Feeney, 1998, Sobotka & Beaujouan 2017, Sobotka 2014).

Results

• The percentage of increase in the number of births by women moved from the group 5-9 years difference varies among the time. • This variation seems to follow the trend of the MAAD. • There is a steady increase of this percentage from 2003 to 2015.

• Mothers and Fathers have increased their age at first kid. • Mothers have a general younger distribution of births and the age span of having children is narrower compare to the fathers. • There are two concentrations on age distribution of births, one before 1980 and the other just right before 2010.

• Difference between the two calculations (factual and counterfactual) is also reducing with the time for the General Fertility Rate (GFR), which also means a reduction in the number of women that are having children with more age-gap. • For the Crude Birth Rate (CBR), remains higher and also the gap between the two tends to be bigger with the time.

• Two non-stationary time series (Dickey-Fuller test p>0.05), MAAD and percentage of increment. Cross-Correlation Function show significant lags in the middle term. Pearson correlation: 0.59. • ACF and PACF, have a strong autoregressive component, and they can be modelled for the same ARIMA (1,2,0) model.

Data & Methods

• Birth registers from 1975 to 2019 collected by INE of Spain.
• A counter factual analysis was used to determine what would happen if women in a higher age-gap group would behave, in terms of the CBR, as the group with less age gap. The formula was given by Stoeckel and Chowdhury (1984).

Discussion & Further Work

• There is a statistical relation between the Mean Absolute Age Difference and the percentage of increase of hypothetical children, however, we cannot conclude causality as it will depend on some biological and social factors not considered in this study.
• To include key periods and stratification variables.
